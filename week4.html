This week is different

Weeks 1 through 3 taught you specific features. Setup, Skills, Connectors, Claude in Chrome. Each one had a clear "here's the tool, here's how to use it." That's necessary, but it's not enough.

The people who get the most out of AI are not waiting for the next training session. They're out in the world, watching what other people are building, reading about new workflows, stumbling across demos and Twitter threads and newsletter breakdowns, and constantly asking one question: "What would I build for my job based on that?"

That reflex is the skill this week teaches. Not a Claude feature. A habit.

Why this matters

AI is moving faster than any company's enablement program can keep up with. New tools ship weekly. New use cases surface daily. The best builders are plugged into the conversation, absorbing from the collective, and translating what they see into action for their own work. They don't wait for permission or a tutorial. They see something, recognize the pattern, and build.

This is how you stay sharp. Not by mastering one tool, but by learning how to learn.

Where to look

Inspiration is everywhere if you know where to look. Newsletters, X threads, YouTube walkthroughs, LinkedIn posts, AI tool demos, Reddit communities, Slack groups. The medium doesn't matter. What matters is that you're subscribed to something, you're paying attention, and you're thinking critically about what you see.

The live conversation around AI workflows is most active on X and Reddit right now. That's where builders share what they're shipping in real time, where new ideas surface fastest, and where the most relevant threads for your day-to-day work are being posted. If you're not there, you're getting the information late.

This week's exercise
  • 1
    Get on X.
    If you already have an account, great. If not, create one. It doesn't need to be personal. You can create an account specifically for following the AI conversation. Screenshot your handle.
  • 2
    Go find inspiration.
    Browse X, Reddit, newsletters, YouTube, AI tool demos, podcasts, or wherever you get your signal. Find 3 examples that are relevant to your work. These can be workflows someone shared, newsletter breakdowns, AI software demos where a feature caught your eye, or anything else where you saw an idea worth borrowing. Your examples can span general productivity and job-specific use cases, but at least 2 of the 3 must be specific to your function. Job-specific examples have a better chance of winning Best in Class.
  • 3
    For each, document the inspiration and translation.
    For all 3 examples, include: what you found (brief description of the idea, workflow, or feature), where you found it (include the link), and what you would build for your job based on this inspiration. The inspiration source must be specific to your function.
  • 4
    Pick your strongest one and build it.
    Take your best inspiration example and actually build the workflow. Record a screen capture of it running, even if it's incomplete or only certain parts are working. Builds that are specific to your job or job family are the strongest candidates for Best in Class.
  • 5
    Share your sources.
    List the accounts, newsletters, podcasts, YouTube channels, or communities you consume for AI and industry content. Call out what you were already following and reading on a weekly basis before this exercise, and what you discovered this week that you plan to follow going forward. Be honest. If you didn't find any of the content useful enough to read on a weekly basis, say that.
  • 6
    Post everything in #ai-unlock10x.
    Your X handle screenshot, your 3 inspiration examples with links and what you'd build, your completed build with screen recording, and your sources list. Once posted, throw a 🤙 on the exercise message to confirm completion.
If you're getting stuck, running into issues, or genuinely can't find inspiration relevant to your role, shoot me a DM and we'll figure it out together.
