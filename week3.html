What we learned last week

Week 2 was about mapping your stack and building your first automation. Looking across all 27 submissions, a clear pattern emerged: the overwhelming majority of workflows followed the same shape. Pull information from a tool, process it, and push a summary to Slack or Gmail. Morning digests, competitor briefings, performance recaps, but fundamentally passive. Claude as a reader and reporter.

That's not a criticism. It's a natural starting point. And it revealed something important about the technology itself.

Connectors have real constraints

Connectors are powerful, but they have two hard limits you start hitting with impactful workflows quickly.

First, coverage. Anthropic supports connectors for a limited set of tools. If your workflow depends on Maven, Sigma, Meta Ads Manager, Google Ads, or Taboola, and for many of you it does, there's no connector. You're stuck.

Second, permissions. Even the connectors that exist are often read-only. Google Drive is the clearest example: Claude can read your Docs, but it cannot read spreadsheets, move files, create new folders, or update a single cell. It can see some things and touch nothing.

This is the wall you'll hit the moment you try to automate anything that actually changes state in the world.

The unlock: Claude in Chrome

Claude in Chrome is a browser extension that opens a browser on your behalf and performs actions in the background while you use other browser windows to work normally. It doesn't need an API. It doesn't need a connector. It navigates, clicks, fills in fields, submits forms, downloads files, and moves data between tabs inside any website you're already logged into.

That means it can work inside Maven. Inside Sigma. Inside your ad accounts. Inside any internal tool your company runs. Anywhere you can go in a browser, Claude in Chrome can go too, and it can take action, not just read.

The builds from last week that pointed here

A handful of Week 2 submissions went beyond summarization. They actually created or updated something rather than just sending a message about it.

Lisa Frendahl built a workflow that found new deals from Slack and automatically appended and sorted them into a live Excel spreadsheet, organized by category. The spreadsheet was the output.

Sumin Oh pulled meeting transcripts from Fellow and published a fully structured synthesis doc directly to Confluence, ready for the team to use. Writing to a knowledge base, not just reporting from one.

Will Oviedo built a morning task aggregator that pulls from six sources and writes new items into a live Google Sheet tracker with status dropdowns and conditional formatting. The sheet stays current throughout the day.

These weren't just smarter digests. They were Claude completing a task that previously required a human to sit at a computer and do it, leaving something built, updated, or filed as proof.

    Extend your audit.
    Go back to your Week 2 tool list. Identify every piece of software you used in the last 30 days that has no MCP connector. List them.
  • 4
    Find 3 action workflows.
    This isn't about dreaming up a new workflow that is now theoretically possible. Think about buttons you already click. What do you do every day or every week that follows a repeatable pattern? Pulling a number from a report and pasting it somewhere. Updating a row when a campaign goes live. Turning off a poor performing publisher. Bringing visibility to a failure or error. Filing a form when a task hits a certain status. Updating a shared team doc with the week's results. The trigger is always the same, the steps are always the same, only the details change. Those are the workflows. They don't need to be complicated. A 3-step process you do every Monday morning is a perfect candidate. Real impact on a weekly basis matters far more than complexity. For each one, name the tool, the trigger, and the specific action.
  • 5
    Build one and record it.
    Before you build, sanity check your chosen workflow with your manager. Some actions carry real downside risk if the details are wrong: an incorrect entry in an ad account, a misrouted form, an overwritten doc. Get alignment before you automate it. If your manager flags a workflow as too risky to automate, call that out in your submission. That's a valid and important finding.
